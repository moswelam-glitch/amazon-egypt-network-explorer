📥 Inbound Operations — Dock to Available

Inbound is everything that happens between a truck arriving at the FC and products becoming "Available" (orderable by customers) in the system. Every second of delay here = later availability = missed sales. The goal: get products from truck to bin as fast and accurately as possible.

Why Inbound matters: A product sitting on a truck or on a staging floor is INVISIBLE to customers. It can't be sold. Every minute in NYR/NYS = lost revenue. Amazon measures "Receive to Available" time religiously.
🎓 The Big Picture: 3-Stage Pipeline
🚛 DOCK→ 📋 RECEIVE→ 📦 STOW
StageInput StateOutput StateWhat Changes
DockTruck at gateItems on floor (NYR)Physical custody transfers from carrier to Amazon
ReceiveNYR (on floor)NYS (on trolley)System acknowledges item exists + validates specs
StowNYS (on trolley)Available (in bin)Item has a home address — now orderable by customers
💡 Think of it like immigration: Dock = landing at airport. Receive = passport control (verified identity). Stow = arriving at your hotel room (assigned address). Until stowed, the item is "homeless" — it exists but no one can find it.

Stage 1: DOCK — Physical Intake

The dock is where physical custody of goods transfers from the carrier (truck driver) to Amazon. This is the highest-risk moment: wrong counts here cascade through the entire system. The TDR process exists to prevent unsafe/uncounted product from entering the building.

🔍 Deep Dive: What is TDR and Why Does It Exist?

TDR (Trailer Door Release) is Amazon's safety gate between "truck at dock" and "start unloading." It exists because:

Risk Without TDRConsequence
Unlicensed driverLiability if accident occurs during unloading
Broken sealTampering — stolen/contaminated product enters inventory
Damaged door hingesDoor falls during unloading — serious injury risk
No safety footwearForklift runs over toes — worker's comp + incident
Wrong trailerReceive wrong vendor's product → inventory contamination
Rule: Even if a truck has been waiting 3 hours and the dock is empty — NO TDR, NO UNLOAD. No exceptions, no overrides, no "just this once." This is a fireable offense to bypass.

Stage 3: STOW — Finding a Home

Stow is the final inbound step. It assigns a permanent address to each item. After stow, the item's status changes to "Available" — customers can now order it. The system knows exactly which bin, shelf, and position it occupies.

Why BOAATS matters: Bad stow = bad pick. Picker can't find item → missed CPT. Picker grabs wrong item → DPMO. Item falls → damaged inventory. BOAATS violations are tracked and audited.

📤 Outbound Operations — Order to Truck

Outbound is everything from "customer clicks Buy" to "package on a truck heading to sort center." This is where Amazon's speed promise is made or broken. Every process is designed to minimize the time between order and dispatch while maintaining zero-defect quality.

Key concept: Outbound works on WAVES. Orders are grouped into waves based on CPT (when they need to leave). Each wave cascades: Pick → Wrangle → Sort → Pack → Dock. Miss a wave = package waits for next one = potential late delivery.

SLAM = Scan, Label, Apply, Manifest. It's a fully automated station. No human judgment needed — pure physics.

📊 ICQA — Inventory Control & Quality Assurance

ICQA is the team that answers one critical question: "Does what the SYSTEM says match what PHYSICALLY exists?" Without ICQA, inventory records drift from reality — leading to phantom orders (customer orders item that doesn't exist), lost items, and financial misstatements. ICQA has two branches: Direct (counting) and Indirect (research/investigation).

Why this matters legally: Amazon is publicly traded. Under SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley Act), inventory records MUST be accurate for financial reporting. IRDR is a SOX-mandated metric. If ICQA fails → audit findings → legal/financial consequences. This isn't optional.

A dedicated team monitors DA behavior for irregular patterns:

Red FlagWhat It IndicatesAction
GPS mismatchDA marks "Delivered" but GPS shows they were elsewhereInvestigation → possible theft/fraud
Excessive "Not Home"DA marking attempts without actually tryingWarning → monitoring → termination
Cash discrepancyCOD collection doesn't match expected amountImmediate investigation
Route deviationDA going to locations not on routeMonitoring + inquiry
Unusually fast completionRoute marked "done" impossibly quicklyVerify all deliveries legitimate
At-risk DA identification: The team also identifies DAs who may be struggling (not malicious) — personal issues, vehicle problems, navigation difficulties — and routes them to support rather than punishment.
